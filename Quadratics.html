Quadratics Model Projectiles

If we throw a ball and want to predict the maximum height achieved, total distance traveled and even how long it would take to reach a certain height or horizontal distance, we can do that! Projectiles follow the path of a parabola, and quadratic equations describe the shape of a parabola. Yep, when you’re playing catch you’re doing a great deal of math and physics without even thinking!

Forms of the Function

The quadratic equation is commonly written or represented in three ways, each of which makes certain features of the curve easier to determine:

Equivalent Forms of the Quadratic Function
Form Expression Tells
Standard y = ax2 + bx + c y-intercept:(0,c),
AOS: x = -b/2a (also gives x ordinate for Vertex)
Factored y = a(x-r)(x-s) Roots: (r,0) and (s,0)
Completed the Square y = a(x-h)2 + k Vertex: (h,k)

FOIL

FOILing is a useful tool for moving between equivalent forms of the quadratic function. While I think of FOIL as my path between all three, some students like to think of FOIL as expanding the factored form to get standard form and use Reverse FOIL to go from standard to factored.

Well after all that, what is FOIL? It stands for multiplying the terms in the order First, Outside, Inside, Last.

Roots

Zeroes, solutions, or roots of a quadratic function are those values of x that result in 0 for y. The co-ordinates of REAL roots take the form (x,0), so these would fall on the x-axis.
